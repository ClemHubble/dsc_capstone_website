Uncertainty Quantification in Large Language Models

Understanding Confidence Across Multiple-Choice, Open-Ended, and Active Reasoning Tasks

Fong Clement Vo • Brooklyn Pagaza • Pranav Singh • Calwin Li • Kening Li

Introduction

Why Does LLM Uncertainty Matter?

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed in high-stakes settings such as healthcare, finance, education, and policy analysis. Despite strong performance, their probabilistic nature means that high confidence does not always imply correctness. Hallucinations, calibration errors, and overconfident reasoning create real-world risks. Robust uncertainty estimation is therefore essential for safe deployment.

Problem Statement

While many uncertainty quantification (UQ) techniques exist, most were designed for standard classification tasks and have not been systematically evaluated across diverse LLM workloads. In particular, it remains unclear how uncertainty behaves across: (1) multiple-choice benchmarks, (2) open-ended generation tasks, and (3) multi-step active reasoning environments.

Our Approach

We conduct a unified empirical study of four complementary UQ methods: Quantile Risk Control (QRC), Sampling with Perturbations (SPUQ), Bayesian Low-Rank Adaptation (BLoB), and Input Clarification Ensembling (ICE). We evaluate calibration, confidence concentration, and robustness across multiple task formats and datasets.

Uncertainty Quantification Techniques

Quantile Risk Control (QRC)

QRC evaluates whether a model’s most confident predictions are truly reliable. It analyzes the upper tail (top 10%) of the confidence distribution and reports: QRC90 (accuracy within top 10%), CVaR90 (mean confidence in top quantile), and EC90 (calibration error among high-confidence predictions).

Sampling with Perturbations (SPUQ)

SPUQ measures robustness by applying controlled perturbations to prompts (paraphrasing, dummy tokens, system message changes). Uncertainty is estimated by observing variability across perturbed responses. Metrics include Expected Calibration Error (ECE), NLL, and Brier score.

Bayesian LoRA (BLoB)

BLoB treats LoRA adapter weights as random variables with Gaussian or Laplace priors. At inference time, multiple weight samples are drawn and predictive uncertainty is estimated from output dispersion. This captures epistemic uncertainty beyond deterministic decoding.

Input Clarification Ensembling (ICE)

ICE generates clarification variants of a question, answers each independently, and aggregates the variability across responses. This decomposes uncertainty induced by ambiguity in natural language inputs and provides calibration metrics including ECE and accuracy.

Key Findings

Conclusion

Our study highlights a clear trade-off between accuracy and calibration across uncertainty quantification techniques. Performance varies significantly depending on task format, with multi-step active reasoning tasks exhibiting greater instability than static QA benchmarks. Reliable uncertainty estimation is therefore task-dependent and essential for safe deployment of LLMs.

Future Work

  • Extend evaluation to larger frontier models.
  • Model full multi-step reasoning pipelines instead of single-turn evaluation.
  • Develop computationally efficient Bayesian calibration techniques.
