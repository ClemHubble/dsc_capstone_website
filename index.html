Can We Trust AI When It Sounds Confident?

Quantifying Uncertainty in Large Language Models

Large language models often sound certain — even when they’re wrong. We study how to measure and improve their reliability.

Why This Matters

AI systems like ChatGPT are increasingly used in education, healthcare, finance, and legal settings. But these models can confidently generate incorrect information — a phenomenon known as hallucination.

If AI is going to assist in high-stakes decisions, it must know when it might be wrong. Our project asks a simple but critical question:

How can we measure how uncertain a language model really is?

[ Illustration: Overconfident AI vs Calibrated AI ]

Our Approach

We evaluated four different uncertainty quantification techniques across three types of tasks:

  • ✔ Multiple-choice question answering
  • ✔ Open-ended text generation
  • ✔ Multi-step reasoning problems

1. Quantile Risk Control (QRC)

Do a model’s most confident answers tend to be correct? QRC focuses on the top 10% most confident predictions and checks whether that confidence is justified.

Technical Details We compute QRC90 (accuracy among top 10%), CVaR90 (mean confidence), and EC90 (calibration error among high-confidence predictions).

2. Sampling with Perturbations (SPUQ)

What happens if we slightly rephrase the question? If the answer changes dramatically, the model may be uncertain.

Technical Details We apply paraphrasing, dummy tokens, and system-message variations and measure variability across outputs.

3. Bayesian LoRA (BLoB)

Instead of treating model weights as fixed, we treat them as probabilistic. This captures deeper uncertainty in the model’s parameters.

Technical Details We sample LoRA adapter weights from Gaussian or Laplace distributions and measure predictive dispersion.

4. Input Clarification Ensembling (ICE)

Sometimes uncertainty comes from ambiguity in the question itself. ICE generates clarification variants and aggregates the responses.

Technical Details We measure variability across clarification-based responses and compute calibration metrics such as ECE.

Impact & Takeaways

Our experiments show that uncertainty behaves differently depending on the type of task. Models tend to be less calibrated in multi-step reasoning tasks compared to static question answering.

There is often a trade-off between accuracy and calibration — improving one can degrade the other.

The key takeaway: reliable uncertainty estimation is essential before deploying language models in high-risk environments.
